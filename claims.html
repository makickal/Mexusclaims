A Comparative Look at Mexican & U.S. Law

An analysis of claims from an email forwarded by Judy D., comparing Mexican law with U.S. federal and state law.

1. No special bilingual programs in schools.

False
Mexico Details

Mexico's Constitution (Art. 2) mandates bilingual and intercultural education to preserve its 68 indigenous national languages. [Source]

U.S. Details

The Supreme Court case Lau v. Nichols (1974) requires schools to assist non-English speaking students, leading to various bilingual and ESL programs. [Source]

U.S. State Considerations

While federally supported, some states have passed "Official English" or "English-only" laws, such as in Arizona and Florida, which can influence public education policies. [Source]

2. All ballots will be in this nation’s language.

Mostly True
Mexico Details

Ballots are primarily in Spanish, but law allows for translators and materials in indigenous languages in relevant communities. [Source]

U.S. Details

Under the federal Voting Rights Act, certain jurisdictions are required to provide bilingual ballots and other language assistance to voters. [Source]

3. Non-citizens cannot vote or hold office.

True
Mexico Details

Constitution (Arts. 34, 35) reserves voting and public office for Mexican citizens, with stricter birth requirements for high office. [Source]

U.S. Details

Federal law and the Constitution reserve voting in federal elections and holding federal office to U.S. citizens. [Source]

U.S. State Considerations

A small number of municipalities in states like Maryland and Vermont allow non-citizens to vote in local elections, but this is not widespread. [Source]

4. Foreigners get no welfare, food stamps, or healthcare.

False
Mexico Details

This is a major distortion. Mexico provides constitutional rights to healthcare/education for all. Legal residents can access public social services. [Source]

U.S. Details

Complex. Undocumented immigrants are ineligible for most federal benefits. Legal immigrants often face a 5-year wait. Emergency services are an exception. [Source]

5. Foreigners face restrictions on buying land.

True
Mexico Details

True. Art. 27 of the Constitution creates a "Restricted Zone" (near coasts/borders) where foreigners use a bank trust (fideicomiso). [Source]

U.S. Details

Generally no federal restrictions on residential property, but reporting requirements exist for foreign investments. [Source]

U.S. State Considerations

An increasing number of states, including Florida and Texas, have laws restricting or banning certain foreign nationals or entities from buying agricultural land or land near military bases. [Source]

6. Foreigners may not protest or criticize the government.

True
Mexico Details

True. Constitution Art. 33 forbids foreigners from interfering in political affairs and grants power to expel those deemed "inconvenient". [Source]

U.S. Details

This is complex. While the First Amendment's rights to speech and assembly apply to non-citizens, visa terms can restrict these activities. Protesting can carry severe immigration risks, including deportation, even for actions protected for citizens. [Source]

7. Illegal immigrants are hunted, jailed, and assets seized.

Exaggerated
Mexico Details

Unauthorized presence is an administrative, not criminal, offense. Asset seizure is not a standard penalty for this violation. [Source]

U.S. Details

Unlawful presence is a civil violation. ICE/CBP enforce laws via detention and removal. Asset forfeiture is a separate legal process, not standard. [Source]

U.S. State Considerations

Some states have passed laws to increase local law enforcement's role in immigration enforcement (e.g., Texas's SB4), while others have "sanctuary" policies limiting cooperation with federal immigration authorities. [Source]
